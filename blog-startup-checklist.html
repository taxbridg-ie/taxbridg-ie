Starting a Business in Ireland: Your Tax Registration Checklist

2 December 2024 6 min read TaxBridge Team

Starting a new business in Ireland is exciting — but getting your tax registrations right from the very beginning will save you significant headaches, penalties, and backdated liabilities later. This checklist covers every Revenue registration you need to consider, whether you are setting up as a sole trader, partnership, or limited company.

Key Takeaways

  • Register for income tax / corporation tax before you start trading
  • VAT registration is mandatory once you exceed €80,000 (goods) or €40,000 (services)
  • Employ staff? You must register as an employer for PAYE/PRSI immediately
  • Limited companies must also file annual returns with the CRO
  • Get professional advice early — it is far cheaper than fixing compliance problems later

Step 1: Choose Your Business Structure

Your tax obligations depend heavily on your business structure. The three main options are:

  • Sole Trader: Simplest structure. You and the business are legally the same. File Form 11 annually. No CRO registration required (unless trading under a business name).
  • Partnership: Two or more individuals trading together. Each partner files their own Form 11. A formal partnership agreement is strongly advised.
  • Limited Company: A separate legal entity. Lower corporation tax rate (12.5% on trading profits). Must register with the CRO, file CT1 returns, and comply with Companies Act obligations.
Which Structure is Right For You?

Many new business owners default to sole trader status because it is simpler. However, if you expect profits above approximately €50,000 per year, a limited company structure can offer significant tax savings. TaxBridge can model this for you before you set up.

The Complete Tax Registration Checklist

☐ Income Tax / Self-Assessment Registration

If you are starting as a sole trader or entering a partnership, register for income tax with Revenue before you begin trading. This gives you a tax reference number and enrols you in self-assessment. You can register via myAccount or ROS.

☐ Corporation Tax Registration

If you are setting up a limited company, you must register for corporation tax with Revenue. You will need your company CRO number to complete this registration. Do this within 30 days of commencing trade.

☐ VAT Registration

You must register for VAT if your annual turnover is expected to exceed:

  • €80,000 for goods
  • €40,000 for services

You can also register voluntarily below these thresholds if it is beneficial for your business (e.g., you have large input VAT costs or your customers are VAT-registered).

☐ Employer PAYE / PRSI Registration

If you plan to hire employees — even one part-time worker — you must register as an employer with Revenue before paying any wages. This registration enables you to operate the PAYE payroll system, deduct income tax, USC, and PRSI from employee salaries, and remit these to Revenue monthly.

Important

Paying wages to an employee before registering as an employer is a serious compliance failure. Revenue can impose significant penalties and the employer becomes personally liable for all unpaid taxes.

☐ Register a Business Name (if applicable)

If you are trading under a name other than your own personal name (as a sole trader), you must register that business name with the Companies Registration Office (CRO). This costs €20 online and is valid for 10 years. You do not need to register a business name if you trade under your own name.

☐ Relevant Contracts Tax (RCT) — Construction Only

If you are operating in the construction, forestry, or meat processing sectors, you must register for Relevant Contracts Tax (RCT) via ROS. This applies to both principal contractors and subcontractors.

☐ Professional Tax Registration

Some regulated professions (solicitors, accountants, medical professionals) have additional registration requirements with their professional bodies. These are separate from Revenue but may have tax implications — consult with TaxBridge to ensure all professional subscription costs are deducted correctly.

Setting Up Good Bookkeeping from Day One

Many business tax problems stem from poor record-keeping rather than malicious intent. From the first day of trading:

  • Open a dedicated business bank account separate from your personal accounts
  • Use accounting software (Xero, QuickBooks, or similar) to track income and expenses
  • Retain all receipts (digital copies are acceptable)
  • Issue proper VAT invoices if VAT-registered
  • Record all cash transactions — Revenue scrutinises businesses that handle significant cash
Pro Tip

Even if you are not required to prepare formal accounts in year one, maintaining clear records from the start saves hours of work when it comes to filing your first tax return — and avoids the costly process of reconstructing records from memory.

Tax Reliefs Available to New Businesses

Irish tax law includes several generous reliefs for new businesses:

  • Start-Up Relief (Companies): Full relief from corporation tax in years 1–3 for new companies with a tax liability below €40,000 per year
  • Entrepreneur Relief: Reduced 10% CGT rate on disposal of a qualifying business (lifetime limit of €1 million)
  • Employment Investment Incentive (EII): Allows investors to claim 30–40% income tax relief on investments in qualifying SMEs
  • Research & Development Tax Credit: 25% credit on qualifying R&D expenditure
