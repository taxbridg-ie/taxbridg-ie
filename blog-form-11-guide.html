Self-Employed in Ireland? Your Complete Guide to Form 11

5 January 2025 8 min read TaxBridge Team

If you are self-employed in Ireland, a proprietary director, or have non-PAYE income, you are required to file a Form 11 — the annual self-assessment tax return. This guide covers who must file, the filing deadline, allowable expenses, and why working with a tax agent like TaxBridge almost always results in a better outcome.

Key Takeaways

  • Form 11 must be filed by 31 October each year (or mid-November if paying via ROS)
  • Self-employed individuals, proprietary directors, and those with non-PAYE income must file
  • Preliminary tax (90% of current year liability) must also be paid by the deadline
  • Significant expenses can be deducted to reduce your taxable profit
  • Late filing results in a 5% surcharge (up to €12,695)

Who Must File a Form 11?

You are required to file a Form 11 if you fall into any of the following categories:

  • Self-employed: Sole traders, freelancers, contractors, and anyone running their own business
  • Proprietary Directors: Directors who own 15% or more of the share capital of a company
  • Non-PAYE Income: If you have rental income, investment income, foreign income, or other non-PAYE income exceeding €5,000
  • PAYE workers with additional income: If your non-PAYE income exceeds the €5,000 threshold
  • Partners in a partnership
First Year Filing

If you become self-employed mid-year, you must register for self-assessment with Revenue promptly. Failure to register is itself a compliance failure. TaxBridge can handle this registration as part of your onboarding.

What Expenses Can You Deduct?

One of the major benefits of self-employment is the ability to deduct legitimate business expenses from your taxable income. Allowable deductions include:

  • Office costs: Rent, rates, utilities, broadband, stationery
  • Staff costs: Salaries, employer PRSI, pension contributions
  • Travel: Business mileage at Revenue rates, public transport for client visits
  • Motor expenses: A proportion of car costs if vehicle is used for business
  • Professional fees: Accountancy, legal, consultancy fees
  • Marketing: Advertising, website, photography, PR
  • Training: Courses and professional development directly related to your business
  • Capital allowances: Wear and tear on equipment (12.5% per year over 8 years)
  • Pension contributions: Relief at marginal rate, age-based limits apply
Common Mistake

Entertainment expenses and client gifts are generally not deductible. Only genuinely business-related costs can be claimed. Revenue auditors pay particular attention to personal expenses being passed through business accounts.

PRSI and USC for the Self-Employed

In addition to income tax, self-employed individuals pay:

  • PRSI: Class S at 4% on all income (minimum annual contribution of €650 applies)
  • USC: Universal Social Charge at graduated rates: 0.5% on first €12,012; 2% up to €22,920; 4% up to €70,044; 8% above €70,044

It is important to set aside enough cash throughout the year to cover your tax liability. A common rule of thumb is to set aside 30–40% of each payment received, depending on your income level.
